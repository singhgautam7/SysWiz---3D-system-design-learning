A **message queue** sits between a part of the system that produces work (**producers**) and
a part that processes it (**consumers**). Instead of a producer calling a consumer directly
and waiting, it drops a message into the queue and moves on. The consumer picks messages up
when it's ready.

<Step n={1} title="Decoupling">

The producer and consumer no longer need to be available at the same moment, or run at the
same speed. In the animation, try raising the producer rate above what the consumer can
handle: the queue **grows**. Lower it, and the queue **drains**. The queue absorbs the
difference — this buffering is the whole point.

</Step>

<Step n={2} title="Why this helps">

- **Smoothing spikes:** a burst of traffic fills the queue instead of overwhelming the consumer.
- **Resilience:** if the consumer briefly goes down, messages wait in the queue rather than being lost.
- **Independent scaling:** add more consumers to drain the queue faster without touching producers.

</Step>

<Callout type="warn">
A queue is a buffer, not infinite storage. If producers consistently outrun consumers, the
queue grows without bound — that's **backpressure** building up. The fix is more/faster
consumers, not a bigger queue. Watch this happen by holding a high producer rate in the
animation.
</Callout>

<KeyTakeaways>
- A message queue decouples producers from consumers in time and speed.
- It smooths spikes, survives brief consumer outages, and lets you scale consumers independently.
- It's a buffer, not infinite storage — sustained overload shows up as an ever-growing queue (backpressure).
</KeyTakeaways>
